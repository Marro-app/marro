## How a non-technical cofounder plugs in

You do not need to code. Here are the areas where a non-technical cofounder adds the most value at this stage. These map directly to what the roadmap needs next.

### 1. User research & the closed beta (highest leverage right now)
The single most important upcoming milestone is a **beta with 20–30 real med students across several schools**, ideally including "strangers of friends" (because close friends are too polite to give honest feedback). You could **own this**: finding participants, scheduling and running interviews, watching how people actually use the app, and turning what you hear into clear notes for Mohamad. The team has literally written down a go/no-go checkpoint based on whether beta users stick around — your research feeds that decision.

### 2. Growth & go-to-market
The strategy is to grow **school by school**, using an **ambassador / founding-member program** (giving engaged students a badge, free premium for life, public credit, and real influence over the product). Building and running that program — recruiting ambassadors, managing invite waves, planning the eventual public launch (e.g. on medical-student communities) — is squarely non-technical cofounder work.

### 3. Operations & company-building
Lots of the near-term to-do list is paperwork and coordination, not engineering:
- Standing up the official company (a Delaware C-corp is the plan), with help from a **free university legal clinic** — the UC Irvine Law Startup & Small Business Clinic (contact on file: Debi Gloria).
- Getting the privacy policy and terms reviewed.
- Keeping company accounts and access tidy and secure (shared password vault, backups, recovery access — so the company doesn't depend on one person).
- Tracking the shared cash-contribution ledger.

### 4. Design & product feedback
Marro follows **Apple's design guidelines** and treats **accessibility** (making the app usable for people with disabilities — e.g. screen-reader users, keyboard-only users, people needing high color contrast) as the **top priority on every change**. You don't need to implement any of this, but a sharp non-technical eye is genuinely valuable: does a screen make sense to a first-time user? Is the wording clear and non-judgmental? Does the flow feel calm? Flagging confusion and rough edges is a real contribution.

### 5. Content, copy & trust
Marro's whole edge is **trust**. There are careful rules about how the app talks about money and data (e.g. never make absolute promises, never make a user feel judged, always label sponsored content clearly, describe benefits not technical plumbing). Helping write and sanity-check user-facing wording — emails, in-app copy, the landing page — is high-impact and needs no code.

## The working rhythm

The intended way of working, from the strategy doc: **mostly asynchronous** (write things down, don't rely on meetings), **one short weekly sync**, loosely-defined ownership of areas, and **the progress log kept up to date** so both founders always know where things stand. PRs (proposed code changes) are the unit of visible progress on the build side.

**A good first week:** read this whole packet, spend real time using the live app as if you were a broke med student, get access to the Notion HQ and the company email, and come to Mohamad with (a) your honest first-impressions of the product and (b) which of the five contribution areas above you're most excited to own.
